A workflow to connect new content automatically

Yes, most of this can run on autopilot. Every surface already publishes a feed the WordPress site can listen to, so a new episode or essay can appear on the Institute, tagged and gated by tier, within minutes of going live. Only Insight Timer stays hands-on.

Where it lives

Where it lives: build on WordPress

Where should the Institute live? I started with what you already run and what our kindred organizations use. Your whole world lives on WordPress: hawahkasat.com and rootstosky.net. The Pocket Project runs its entire school (courses, practice groups, events) on WordPress; Yoga for Humankind pairs a WordPress site with a separate trainings portal. My recommendation is to build on the WordPress you already own, since it keeps the community and, later, the courses under one roof. Circle is a strong alternative if you would rather have the most turnkey community from day one.

🌐 WordPress (recommended)

A school on land you already own
What it is
The Institute built into the WordPress you already run, with a course plugin (LifterLMS or LearnDash) and a membership layer. The explorer lives natively, and everything sits on your own domain.
Why it fits you
Full ownership of members, data, and home, with the lowest long-term cost. And it is the proven path of your peers: the Pocket Project runs entirely on WordPress, and Yoga for Humankind's WordPress-plus-portal model shows the same road.
What it costs
Roughly $300–600/yr in plugins on hosting you already pay for; no per-member fees beyond Stripe's.
What it asks
A steward to tend updates, security, and support, and the community spaces need deliberate care to feel like a hearth rather than a comment thread. Live circles run through Zoom links, since nothing live is built in.
Choose this if
Ownership and long-term economics lead. The school comes first, and the sangha grows around it at its own pace.

🔥 Circle (alternative)

A hearth with a school inside it
What it is
A community platform (Circle) where the sangha, the pathways, live circles, and payments share one roof. The commons opens on day one with no course attached: the sangha is native here, not bolted on.
Why it fits you
Calm, spaces-based, and nothing to maintain. Your fire keepers and space holders get rooms of their own, the episode-map explorer embeds cleanly, live streams and small live rooms are built in, and members are unlimited on every plan.
What it costs
$89/mo annual (Professional) to $199/mo (Business), plus 0.5–2% fees on top of Stripe's. July 2026 pricing, reconfirm at signup.
What it asks
A subscription for as long as the Institute lives, and the home is leased: members and content are exportable, but the land is Circle's.
Choose this if
The sangha is the product. Belonging comes first, and courses are how belonging deepens.
The one question that decides it: is the Everlutionary Institute first a sangha with a school inside it, or a school with a sangha growing around it? Either home can open the first season within a few months, and while the founding circle is small, moving between them later is very doable. My recommendation is WordPress, since it keeps the community and, later, the courses under one roof you own. Circle stays a strong alternative if the turnkey community matters more than ownership. This one is yours to feel into, and I will build either.

Paths considered and set aside for now: staying on Patreon alone (chats and livestreams now, but no course structure and a scattered member experience); a Kajabi portal on a subdomain (Yoga for Humankind's exact model, but ~$149+/mo buys marketing machinery we don't need yet); a course-first LMS like Thinkific or Teachable (a catalog, when what we need is a commons); and building the school inside Insight Timer (a mighty free funnel, and it stays exactly that: they own the members, so it remains the gift, not the home). Membership plumbing

Using your Patreon on the WordPress site

Patreon publishes an official WordPress plugin that lets patrons sign up on Patreon and unlock WordPress-hosted content by tier. People still pledge on Patreon, and the site simply checks their tier and opens the right doors. Your founding circle stays intact, and it fits the gift economy, since Patreon supports free and pay-what-you-can tiers.

How it works

The official Patreon WordPress plugin connects your Patreon account to the site. On any post, page, practice, or course you set a minimum tier. Visitors below that tier see an "Unlock with Patreon" button that sends them to Patreon to pledge and returns them to the unlocked content. Existing patrons log in with Patreon once, and from then on their access follows their pledge.

The steps

  1. Install the official plugin. In WordPress go to Plugins, Add New, search "Patreon WordPress" by Patreon, Inc., then Install and Activate.
  2. Connect in two clicks. Run the plugin's setup wizard and authorize it with your Patreon account. It links the two and pulls in your tiers automatically.
  3. Gate content by tier. On any post, page, practice, or course, choose the minimum tier from the plugin's dropdown and update. Everything at or above that tier unlocks for those patrons.
  4. Decide what stays open. Leave the sangha welcome, the free breath, and public episode excerpts ungated, so newcomers always meet the gift before any ask.
  5. Let patrons in. New visitors pledge through the "Unlock with Patreon" button and land back on the unlocked page. Returning patrons just log in with Patreon and the site recognizes their tier.
  6. For courses later. When discrete courses arrive on WordPress, gate each course or lesson by the same tiers. For finer control such as drip release or completion tracking, pair the plugin with a membership layer like Paid Memberships Pro, which it is built to work alongside.
The honest read: Patreon keeps the billing and takes its platform fee, 8 to 12 percent depending on your plan, plus payment processing, and access depends on the Patreon login staying connected. That is the trade for not rebuilding the membership. If you later want to own billing outright, a native WordPress membership such as Paid Memberships Pro or WooCommerce with Stripe can replace or sit beside Patreon, though patrons would need to re-subscribe. Most creators start with the Patreon plugin and revisit only if the fee or the dependency begins to pinch.

A design ethic

The gift economy as the operating model

The matrix names the Institute's sharpest contradiction: the wellness field mostly sells individual optimization, and your own work (American Detox, No Kings) refuses that. Kazu Haga, your EP43 guest, has spent years building the alternative. His gift economy is not a discount or a "no fee" gesture; it is a whole way of holding resources, drawn from natural ecosystems and Buddhist dana. It can be the Institute's operating model rather than an afterthought.

"The Gift Economy, to me, is a system of managing resources in a way that is aligned with our natural ecosystem. For me, this is about so much more than choosing to not charge a fee."
Kazu Haga, kazuhaga.com/gifteconomics. Nature manages resources in balance, and our essential nature is cooperation, not competition.
PRINCIPLE 01

Generosity

Giving without expectation of return. Not a transaction, but a transformation of culture and relationship.
In the Institute: Every Insight Timer practice and the open sangha are given freely. The invitation to give comes after the gift, never as the gate in front of it.

Access

A price tag is a barrier. All are welcome regardless of ability to pay.
In the Institute: The core pathway runs on a sliding scale with a real $0 door. No one is turned away for lack of funds; the founding circle underwrites seats for those who cannot pay.
PRINCIPLE 03

Interdependence

Needs are held collectively, not by each person alone.
In the Institute: Members give in support of people they will never meet. A fixed share of every contribution funds scholarships and the land, so the community carries each other's needs.
PRINCIPLE 04

Sustainability

Enough for everyone's need, not everyone's greed. Bigger is not better.
In the Institute: Success is not measured by budget size. The Institute grows to meet need, not to chase the largest grant. Seasonal rhythm over a publishing treadmill protects the ecosystem and your rest.
PRINCIPLE 05

Abundance

Resources are abundant when we count water, soil, relationships, and wisdom, not only money.
In the Institute: The Institute leads from what it already holds: 44+ conversations, a plural lineage, 125 acres, a devoted community. It builds from abundance, not scarcity.
PRINCIPLE 06

Equity

Honor each person's story and capacity. Invite those who can to give beyond their fair share.
In the Institute: The Sustainer, Fire Keeper, and Space Holder tiers become gift tiers, not paywalls: those with capacity give more so others give nothing. Reparative by design.
PRINCIPLE 07

Transparency

Counter secrecy with radical financial openness. Transparency builds trust.
In the Institute: Publish where the money goes: build, teachers, land, scholarships. Trust becomes the Institute's infrastructure.
PRINCIPLE 08

Faith

Rely on relationships, not fundraising machinery. If the path is right, community sustains it.
In the Institute: Launch to the founding circle and trust them to hold the work. Pour energy into the teaching, not into funnels, and let the sangha be the fundraising.
The honest read: the gift economy is not cost-free; it is a bet on relationship, and it carries real risk to cash flow. Hold it with two supports. First, a sustaining base: the founding-circle pre-sale, a Sustainer gift tier for those with capacity, and Roots to Sky's existing Give structure, so the faith is informed, not blind. Kazu is the natural advisor for designing this model, which is one more reason to invite him in early as a fire keeper.
Implementation

How the connection works, in detail

Each one builds directly on something already working, and each carries its moment on the timeline. The sangha comes first: we tend the fire before we open the doors.

IDEA 01
Begins now · month 0, before launch

Sangha open from day one

The community space is never gated behind a course, and it opens before anything else. We develop the sangha rhythms now: welcome rituals, weekly prompts, story circles, dedications, and the first space holders drawn from your most devoted members, so people arrive into a living room, not an empty hall.

Why first: belonging is the product; courses are how belonging deepens. A warm commons at launch is what makes every other idea land.
IDEA 02
Months 1–3 · runs with every episode

The alchemy of one conversation

Every conversation transmutes into three artifacts sized to proven attention: a 13-minute practice (Insight Timer, free), a 35-minute teaching (module core), an hour-long dialogue (flagship + live circle). One recording, three doors.

Why now: your own timestamps already prove all three containers work. This is alchemy, not new production. Three solo episodes are transcribed and ready to transmute first.
IDEA 03
Blessings now · circles from month 3

Guests become fire keepers

The most-watched episodes are dialogues with natural co-teachers: Prentis Hemphill, Edgar Villanueva, Hilary Giovale, Ariel Clark. Invite each to bless a module or hold space for one live circle a year, tending the flame with you.

Why now: it shares the load you can't carry alone, and the audience has already chosen these voices. The asks go out early so the calendar can breathe.
IDEA 04
Months 2–4 · as the first modules land

The episode-map explorer

An interactive, explorable map of the archive: every episode a node that opens into its theme, practice, reflection, and guest's wisdom.

Why then: 44+ episodes is a living archive nobody else has; this makes it feel like one body of work instead of a feed, once the first modules prove the shape.
IDEA 05
Invitation open always · no set timeline

The return to the land

Every pathway carries an open invitation to Roots to Sky: ceremony, land, in-person sangha, for those who feel called. Never a requirement, never a finish line. A share of Institute proceeds feeds the Sanctuary, so learning funds regeneration even for those who never make the trip.

Why gently: no online peer owns a healing land base, and it keeps the sacred off the internet. It also carries the most logistics, so it stays a light invitation until the sangha's own longing sets the pace.
